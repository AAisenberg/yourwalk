# YourWalk Stakeholder Map

This document maps key stakeholders for the YourWalk pilot, their needs, concerns, and what success looks like for each group.

## Stakeholder Groups

### Council Stakeholders

#### Project Sponsor
**Who**: Senior Council staff responsible for grant and project oversight

**Needs**:
- Project delivers on grant commitments
- Evidence of community value and engagement
- Clear path to potential scale or continuation
- Grant reporting requirements met

**Likely Concerns**:
- Project timeline and budget
- Community engagement success
- Data quality and reliability
- Long-term sustainability

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Pilot completes on time and within budget
- Strong community participation and positive feedback
- Useful insights for Council planning
- Grant reporting demonstrates value
- Foundation for potential scale or continuation

---

#### Communications Team
**Who**: Council staff responsible for community engagement and communications

**Needs**:
- Clear messaging about the tool and its purpose
- Materials for community engagement (web page, posters, social media)
- Guidance on how to promote participation
- Support for community questions and feedback

**Likely Concerns**:
- Community response and participation rates
- Managing expectations about tool capabilities
- Handling sensitive feedback or reports
- Maintaining engagement over pilot period

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Community engagement materials are clear and effective
- Good participation rates and positive community response
- Community questions handled smoothly
- Engagement sustains throughout pilot period

---

#### Transport and Urban Design Staff
**Who**: Council staff responsible for transport planning and urban design

**Needs**:
- Evidence-based insights about walking network performance
- Data to support infrastructure prioritisation
- Identification of problem areas and corridors
- Exportable data for use in planning processes

**Likely Concerns**:
- Data accuracy and reliability
- Alignment with existing planning priorities
- Usability of insights for decision-making
- Integration with existing planning tools and processes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Insights identify areas that align with known issues
- Insights reveal previously unknown problem areas
- Data supports infrastructure prioritisation decisions
- Exports are useful for reports and planning documents

---

#### Assets Team
**Who**: Council staff responsible for footpath, lighting, and crossing infrastructure

**Needs**:
- Data about asset condition and performance
- Community feedback about asset issues
- Evidence to support maintenance and upgrade priorities
- Integration with asset management systems (future)

**Likely Concerns**:
- Data accuracy compared to official asset records
- Duplication with existing asset management processes
- Resource implications of addressing identified issues
- Liability concerns about community-reported issues

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Community data complements official asset records
- Identifies maintenance priorities that align with asset management plans
- Provides evidence for upgrade and maintenance decisions
- No liability issues from community-reported data

---

### Community Stakeholders

#### General Residents
**Who**: Casey residents who walk for transport, recreation, or exercise

**Needs**:
- Easy-to-use tool for planning walking routes
- Clear, understandable information about route conditions
- Confidence that route recommendations are reliable
- Tool that works on their devices (mobile, tablet, desktop)

**Likely Concerns**:
- Privacy and data security
- Accuracy of route recommendations
- Understanding how to use the tool
- Whether the tool will be maintained long-term

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Tool is easy to use and understand
- Route recommendations help them choose better routes
- They trust the information provided
- They would use the tool again and recommend it to others

---

#### Parents and Carers
**Who**: Parents, carers, and people walking with children or prams

**Needs**:
- Information about route accessibility for prams and children
- Safety information (lighting, crossings, traffic)
- Routes that are suitable for family walking
- Confidence that routes are safe for children

**Likely Concerns**:
- Safety of recommended routes
- Accessibility for prams and strollers
- Understanding safety limitations (no guarantees)
- Privacy when planning routes with children

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Tool helps them find routes suitable for families
- They feel more confident planning routes with children
- Accessibility information is accurate and useful
- They contribute observations about family-friendly routes

---

#### People with Mobility Needs
**Who**: People using wheelchairs, mobility scooters, walking aids, or with other accessibility requirements

**Needs**:
- Accurate accessibility information for routes
- Confidence that routes are actually accessible
- Ability to contribute accessibility observations
- Tool that is itself accessible (WCAG compliant)

**Likely Concerns**:
- Accuracy of accessibility data
- Whether routes are truly accessible (not just "technically" accessible)
- Tool accessibility (can they use it with assistive technology?)
- Whether their contributions are valued and used

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Accessibility data is accurate and helps them plan accessible routes
- Tool is fully accessible and usable with assistive technology
- Their contributions are valued and improve data quality
- They feel the tool addresses their specific needs

---

#### Schools and Community Groups
**Who**: Schools, community centres, walking groups, accessibility advocacy groups

**Needs**:
- Tool that supports their community activities
- Ability to contribute observations as a group
- Data that supports advocacy for infrastructure improvements
- Materials to promote tool use within their networks

**Likely Concerns**:
- Whether the tool will be used by their community
- How to encourage participation
- Whether data will lead to actual infrastructure improvements
- Long-term availability of the tool

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Tool is promoted and used within their networks
- Group contributions improve data coverage
- Data supports advocacy efforts
- They see value in continued use

---

## Stakeholder Engagement Plan

### Regular Communication

- **Weekly updates**: Brief status updates to project sponsor and key Council staff
- **Sprint reviews**: Demo completed work, gather feedback
- **Monthly reports**: Progress, usage metrics, engagement updates
- **Quarterly reviews**: Strategic review with project sponsor

### Key Touchpoints

- **Project kickoff**: Align on goals, timeline, expectations
- **MVP launch**: Demo and training for Council staff
- **Beta launch**: Community engagement launch, Council training
- **v1 launch**: Public launch, demo day, showcase
- **Evaluation**: Share findings, gather feedback for future

### Feedback Channels

- **Council staff**: Direct feedback during sprint reviews, email, meetings
- **Community**: In-app survey, end-of-pilot survey, email, Council contact
- **Delivery team**: Sprint retrospectives, team meetings, documentation

## Managing Conflicting Needs

### Potential Conflicts

- **Council vs Community**: Council may want different features than community
- **Accessibility vs Performance**: Detailed accessibility data may impact performance
- **Privacy vs Insights**: More data improves insights but raises privacy concerns
- **Scope vs Timeline**: More features vs faster delivery

### Resolution Approach

- **Prioritise user value**: Focus on features that deliver most value to end users
- **Transparent trade-offs**: Document decisions and rationale
- **Iterative improvement**: Start with MVP, improve based on feedback
- **Stakeholder input**: Regular feedback loops to align priorities
